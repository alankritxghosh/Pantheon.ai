# Q3 Business Review: Pantry Restock

## Problem Statement

Pantry Restock was launched to make repeat grocery purchases easier for households that buy the same shelf-stable items every month. The Q3 business result is mixed. The program increased repeat purchase frequency among enrolled customers, but it also created new customer confusion about why an item appeared, disappeared, or changed quantity in a suggested basket.

The service is useful when it behaves like a thoughtful reminder. It becomes risky when it behaves like an unexplained subscription. Customers want help remembering coffee filters, pet food, cereal, and cleaning supplies. They do not want the company to appear to assume authority over household purchasing. The difference is subtle, and our current experience does not always respect it.

The review question is whether Pantry Restock should continue expanding, pause for trust improvements, or narrow to categories where repeat intent is strongest.

## Tenets

The first tenet is that replenishment must remain a suggestion. The customer should understand that the system is helping them remember, not placing household demand on rails. Language that sounds automatic should be removed unless the customer has explicitly opted into automation.

The second tenet is that quantity changes require explanation. If the system recommends two units instead of one because usage appears higher, the product should say why in plain language. Silent quantity changes create a feeling of loss of control.

The third tenet is that repeat behavior is not always preference. A customer may buy the same paper towels twice because they were on promotion, not because the brand is permanent. The model should be conservative before treating repeated purchases as durable intent.

The fourth tenet is that the program should earn automation one step at a time. Reminder, suggested basket, one-click approval, and scheduled order are different trust levels. The customer should graduate through them deliberately.

## Customer Experience

The customer opens Northstar Grocery and sees a small Pantry Restock module below the primary basket. It says, "Items you may be running low on," not "Your restock basket." The module shows four suggested items with short reasons: "usually bought every five weeks" or "last purchased in a larger pack."

The customer can approve, snooze, or remove each item. Snoozing matters because it teaches the system without forcing the customer to reject the product. Removing an item asks for no survey. We should not turn a household preference correction into a form.

When the customer approves several suggestions, the system creates a basket section, not a separate order. The customer can still compare promotions and delivery windows. If a suggested item is unavailable, the product does not substitute automatically unless the customer has already approved that category's substitution rules.

After checkout, the receipt distinguishes normal basket items from restock suggestions. This gives the customer a memory of what the system influenced and reduces the risk that they feel tricked later.

## Working Backwards

Press release draft: Northstar is improving Pantry Restock with clearer suggestions, customer-controlled quantity changes, and a path from reminders to optional automation. The update keeps the convenience customers liked in the first release while making the system's reasoning easier to inspect.

The customer benefit is confidence. Instead of receiving a mysterious basket, customers see why an item is suggested and can approve or dismiss it quickly. The business benefit is healthier retention. A smaller number of trusted suggestions is more valuable than a larger number of suggestions that customers second-guess.

The Q4 plan should focus on trust instrumentation before category expansion. We should earn the right to automate more, not assume it from repeat purchase data alone.

## FAQs

**Did Pantry Restock improve repeat purchase behavior?** Yes. Enrolled households purchased restock categories more frequently than comparable non-enrolled households. The issue is that support contacts also increased around unexpected suggestions and quantity changes.

**Should we pause expansion?** We should pause broad expansion and continue in categories with strong repeat intent. Pet food, filters, and cleaning supplies are better candidates than snacks or promotional pantry items.

**Is scheduled ordering still part of the strategy?** Yes, but it should be a later trust level. The near-term experience should emphasize suggestions and one-click approval.

**What is the main Q4 metric?** The leading metric should be approved suggestions per active household with no increase in "unexpected item" contacts. Repeat purchase rate alone is not sufficient.

## Appendix

Q3 support themes were "I did not add this," "why did quantity change," and "I bought this once for a party." These themes are not defects in isolation; they are signals that the product language grants the system more authority than the customer intended. The recommendation is to continue Pantry Restock with a narrower category scope, revised copy, and explicit quantity explanations before the holiday grocery peak.
